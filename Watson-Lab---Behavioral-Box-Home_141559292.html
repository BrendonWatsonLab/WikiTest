Introduction

Acquisition of behavioral and cellular data in the mouse is integral to the success of neurobiological experiments with various aims and areas of study. The ability to do so in the mouse home environment eliminates the need to transfer the mouse from the home cage to the experimental environment for each iteration of experiments. Murine stress tests are often conducted in an effort to study depression-like behavior in rodents with little comprehensive study of behavior enduring beyond these tests in the murine home environment. We aimed to develop an automated method of reliably tracking mouse behavior in their home cage, allowing for many of the behaviors currently associated with depressive symptoms in humans to be studied in mice. The core system consists of an infrared camera combined with the Cleversys mouse behavior analysis system. In addition to video data, food pellet consumption, water consumption, and running wheel revolutions are time-stamped and recorded. A more comprehensive analysis of behaviors and behavioral patterns may reveal behavioral phenotypes characteristic of depression, and provide further insight to a more delineated model of murine stress. The added ability to acquire physiological data in vivo in conjunction with this more comprehensive behavioral analysis may provide a more robust experimental design with which to study the behavioral and physiological components of murine stress, potentially leading to a symptomatic guideline of rodent depression.

Design

Our behavioral box contains an infrared LED backlight that remains lit, placed behind an acrylic diffuser.. This light falls below the perceivable range of our mice, but enables us to continuously record behavior using our camera. Video recordings enable the recognition and classification of over 40 different behaviors with the Cleversys mouse behavior analysis system while retaining the raw footage for later analysis. To control a circadian light cycle during the 12-hour “day," a constant 300 lux warm yellow LED strip is active to simulate natural sunlight conditions. The mouse is otherwise isolated from external light sources, but is provided continuous air circulation via a light-tight louver vent and 120mm light-tight fan. These considerations were made not only to control the circadian light cycle, but also in order to maintain temperature, ammonium levels, and humidity to an acceptable level accounting for the heat produced by the various electronic components and light sources located in the cage, and the housing of the mouse in the environment. The mouse is given ad libitum access to food and water via infrared "nose-poke" sensors.  The infrared beam is broken by the insertion of the mouse's nose into a "port" in the side of the cage. The time and duration of these events are recorded, and food or water are dispensed from the port. The food is dispensed in the form of a small pellet <SOURCE> and the water dispensed in user-configurable increments. A timeout can be placed on sequential dispense events to prevent excess food or water from being dispensed into the cage by a continual nose-poke. An exercise wheel with an infrared sensor recording revolutions is also outfitted in the behavioral box, whose beam is broken by a reflective portion of the wheel covering 180 degrees of rotation in the wheel. The infrared sensors and power sources for the solenoids were all designed to be modular — each infrared sensor can be disconnected from the Arduino and replaced if necessary to increase the ease of user serviceability.

Performance

The ability to monitor the time and quantity of dispersal of sucrose water, regular water, control pellets, and high-fat food pellets provides a uniquely thorough observation of mouse behavior in the home cage when combined with information from the revolutions of the exercise wheel and additional behavioral analysis from infrared video recording and the Cleversys mouse behavior analysis system. The Cleversys system enables the recognition and classification of over 40 different behaviors, while also retaining the raw footage for later analysis. The behavior box further provides information on both request and dispense time for the pellet and water options, all allowing for potential temporal pairing with cellular data acquired in vivo.
